The intricate interactions between inflammasomes and bacterial pathogens: Roles, mechanisms, and therapeutic potentials
Pharmacology & Therapeutics, 2024
DOI: 10.1016/j.pharmthera.2024.108756
Role of Inflammasomes in Bacterial Infections: Unveiling the Mechanisms and Therapeutic Potential
 
Prof. Eun-Kyeong Jo from CNU and her team explored the complex interactions between inflammasomes and bacterial pathogens, shedding light on their roles in immunity and infection. The study reveals how bacteria exploit inflammasome pathways to evade immune responses and persist within hosts, highlighting the therapeutic potential of harnessing bacterial immune subversion strategies to tackle drug-resistant bacterial infections.

Arenicolide Family Macrolides Provide a New Therapeutic Lead Combating Multidrug-Resistant Tuberculosis
Angewandte International Edition Chemie, 2024
DOI: 10.1002/anie.202412994
Arenicolides as Potential Drug Leads Against Drug-Resistant Tuberculosis (TB)
 
A research team led by Prof. Eun-Kyeong Jo discovered eight new arenicolide compounds from Micromonospora sp. GR10, including arenicolide A (Ar–A), which showed potent antimicrobial activity against multidrug-resistant (MDR) and extensively drug-resistant (XDR) Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) strains. Ar–A effectively exerted an antimicrobial effect against Mtb in macrophages, demonstrated therapeutic potential in Mtb infections, and exhibited a synergistic effect with amikacin in a mouse model of MDR-TB lung infection. These findings highlight Ar–A as a promising drug lead for drug-resistant TB.

Ubiquitin regulatory X (UBX) domain-containing protein 6 is essential for autophagy induction and inflammation control in macrophages
Cellular & Molecular Immunology, 2024
DOI: 10.1038/s41423-024-01222-1
Regulation of Autophagy and Inflammation by UBXN6 in Human Sepsis
 
Researchers from CNU report ubiquitin regulatory X domain-containing protein 6 (UBXN6) as a pivotal regulator of inflammatory responses via the activation of autophagy in humans with sepsis. The study shows that UBXN6 depletion in macrophages leads to exacerbated inflammation, increased mitochondrial oxidative stress, impaired autophagy, and metabolic shifts that disrupt immune function, highlighting UBXN6's crucial role in immune regulation during sepsis.

Targeting ERR promotes cytotoxic effects against acute myeloid leukemia through suppressing mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation
Journal of Hematology & Oncology, 2022
DOI: 10.1186/s13045-022-01372-7
ERR Regulation of Mitochondrial Oxidative Phosphorylation in Acute Myeloid Leukemia
 
A research team at CNU explored the role of estrogen-related receptor- (ERR) in acute myeloid leukemia (AML) and its impact on mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (mtOXPHOS). The study shows that elevated ERR levels are linked to worse clinical outcomes in patients with AML. Blocking ERR using the pharmacologic inhibitor (XCT-790) or gene silencing suppressed mtOXPHOS and enhanced anti-leukemic effects both in vitro and in mouse models.

The dual role of autophagy in acute myeloid leukemia
Journal of Hematology & Oncology, 2022
DOI: 10.1186/s13045-022-01262-y
Dual Role of Autophagy in Acute Myeloid Leukemia: Tumorigenesis and Therapeutic Implications
 
CNU researchers explored the dual role of autophagy in acute myeloid leukemia (AML), highlighting its complex involvement in both tumor initiation and progression, as well as anticancer responses. The study reviews recent findings on autophagy-related genes as potential prognostic markers and therapeutic targets in AML. It also discusses the interplay between autophagy and tumor cell metabolism, along with the development of autophagy modulators as potential treatments. The review emphasizes the need for a deeper understanding of autophagy's dual functions in AML to guide future therapeutic strategies.

Arginine-mediated gut microbiome remodeling promotes host pulmonary immune defense against nontuberculous mycobacterial infection
Gut Microbes, 2022
DOI: 10.1080/19490976.2022.2073132
Dynamics of Arginine-Mediated Gut Microbiome Remodeling in Pulmonary Defense Against Nontuberculous Mycobacterial Infection
 
Researchers at CNU collaborated with institutes across the globe and discovered that oral L-arginine supplementation enhances pulmonary immunity against nontuberculous mycobacterial pulmonary diseases (NTM-PDs) by reshaping the gut microbiome. The treatment promoted protective immune responses in the lungs and increased levels of Bifidobacterium pseudolongum, further boosting the defense against NTM infections, including multidrug-resistant strains.

Chemical modulation of SQSTM1/p62-mediated xenophagy that targets a broad range of pathogenic bacteria
Autophagy, 2022
DOI: 10.1080/15548627.2022.2054240
Chemical Modulation of SQSTM1/p62-Mediated Xenophagy for Targeting Pathogenic Bacteria
 
CNU researchers, along with researchers from Korea, developed chemical mimics of the N-degron pathway to induce targeted degradation of intracellular bacteria, including Salmonella, Escherichia coli, Streptococcus pyogenes, and Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb). These chemicals bind to the SQSTM1/p62 protein, promoting the biogenesis and recruitment of autophagic membranes for bacterial degradation. The treatment demonstrated antimicrobial efficacy in vitro and in mice, including against multidrug-resistant Mtb, and reduced inflammatory cytokine production. This approach shows potential as a host-directed therapeutic for a wide range of drug-resistant bacterial infections.

An update on the regulatory mechanisms of NLRP3 inflammasome activation
Cellular & Molecular Immunology, 2021
DOI: 10.1038/s41423-021-00670-3
Regulatory Mechanisms of NOD-, LRR-, and Pyrin Domain-Containing Protein 3 (NLRP3) Inflammasome Activation
 
Researchers at CNU examine the molecular mechanisms underlying NLRP3 inflammasome activation, with a focus on the priming (Signal 1) and assembly (Signal 2) processes. The review highlights recent findings on the transcriptional and posttranslational regulation of NLRP3, including protein-protein interactions and spatiotemporal regulation. By outlining both positive and negative regulatory pathways that influence inflammasome activation, this work enhances understanding of NLRP3s role in various diseases and suggests potential therapeutic avenues for targeting inflammasome-related conditions.

MiR-144-3p is associated with pathological inflammation in the patients infected with Mycobacteroides abscessus
Experimental & Molecular Medicine, 2021
DOI: 10.1038/s12276-020-00552-0
Role of MiR-144-3p in Pathological Inflammation During Mycobacteroides abscessus Infection
 
CNU researchers, along with Sungkyunkwan University School of Medicine and Chonnam National University, investigated the immune profiles of patients infected with Mycobacteroides abscessus subsp. abscessus and M. abscessus subsp. massiliense, revealing that miR-144-3p is significantly upregulated in peripheral blood mononuclear cells. The study found a strong correlation between miR-144-3p expression and increased levels of proinflammatory cytokines/chemokines, linking the microRNA to pathological inflammation during infection. Overexpression of miR-144-3p promoted inflammation and bacterial growth in macrophages, suggesting its potential role in M. abscessus-associated inflammatory responses and bacterial pathogenesis.

ESRRA (estrogen related receptor alpha) is a critical regulator of intestinal homeostasis through activation of autophagic flux via gut microbiota
Autophagy, 2021
DOI: 10.1080/15548627.2020.1847460
Role of Estrogen Related Receptor Alpha (ESRRA) in Intestinal Homeostasis
 
Researchers at CNU explored the role of ESRRA in intestinal homeostasis. The study demonstrates that ESRRA regulates autophagic flux and gut microbiota, protecting against colonic inflammation. In Esrra-deficient mice, increased susceptibility to dextran sodium sulfate-induced colitis, mitochondrial dysfunction, and altered gut microbiota were observed. Fecal microbiota transplantation from wild-type mice alleviated colitis symptoms, and reduced ESRRA expression in the intestinal mucosa was associated with increased disease activity in ulcerative colitis patients. These findings underscore the critical role of ESRRA in maintaining intestinal health and controlling inflammation.
