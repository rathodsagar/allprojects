Unlocking the Secrets of Magnesium Alloy Anisotropy Using Artificial Intelligence
Magnesium alloys have a wide range of applications owing to their great properties, but often exhibit anisotropic deformation behavior, reducing durability. Associate Professor Taekyung Lee from the School of Mechanical Engineering, along with his team has developed a machine learning-based model for accurately predicting such deformation with excellent reliability. This study can assist in the designing of magnesium alloy products with complex plastic anisotropy.

Novel Additives for Enhancing Perovskite Solar Cells
Lead halide perovskites have emerged as promising materials for next-generation high-performance solar cells. A team of researchers led by Prof. Hyo Jung Kim from the School of Chemical Engineering and Prof. Ji Youn Seo from the Department of Nano Fusion Technology developed a new approach, using additives, to improve the stability and power conversion efficiency of lead halide perovskites. The study provides an in-depth understanding of designing and selecting additives for increased efficiency and stability of perovskite solar cells.

Improving the Stability of Perovskite Solar Cells
Perovskite solar cells, with high power conversion energy, have the potential to replace traditional silicon solar cells but suffer from degradation due to moisture. A team of researchers led by Professor of Nanoenergy Engineering, Ji-Youn Seo utilized crown ethers for interfacial passivation, resulting in improved stability while maintaining power conversion efficiency.

Study Reveals a Brand New Hall Effect
The Hall effect is an important phenomenon, in which a voltage is generated when both electric and magnetic fields are applied to materials. It is used for sensing applications in many industries. Prof. Choongyu Hwang from the Department of Physics, and his team explored a new type of Hall effect, called the non-linear Hall effect. This study provides insights into room temperature non-linear Hall effect materials, paving the way towards innovative quantum devices.

Novel 4D LIDAR Sensor Technology
Professor Chang-Seok Kim’s research team at the Department of Optics and Mechatronics Engineering and electromagnetic energy materials research team at Hyundai Motor Company’s Basic Materials Research Center developed a novel LIDAR sensor technology that enables autonomous vehicles to see even in bad weather. The novel technology reduces environmental interference and inter-vehicle interference.
