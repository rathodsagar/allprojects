Mg–Ti composites are highly applicable in aerospace, future mobility, biomedical, and other high-tech industries due to their high specific strength, biocompatibility, and multifunctionality. The liquid metal dealloying (LMD) process is an innovative method for fabricating three-dimensional bicontinuous metal–metal composites, including Mg–Ti composites.

Our research lab has developed a novel two-step LMD process to produce high-strength composites with a three-dimensional interconnected microstructure. Porous Ti is fabricated through LMD (step 1), then immersed in a Mg-Al melt (step 2), producing  a three-phase composite (Mg–Ti3Al–TiAl). This composite has 1.8 times higher hardness and 1.3 times higher strength than a Mg–Ti composite made by the conventional LMD process.
 
 
Magnesium (Mg) is the lightest structural metallic material, and Mg alloys have attracted considerable interest in various industries due to their low density. However, conventional Mg alloys exhibit low maximum extrusion speeds, which increase processing costs and restrict their widespread applications.

Our research lab has developed high-extrudable and high-strength Mg–Bi–Al alloys, free of expensive alloying elements. These Mg–Bi–Al alloys can be extruded at high speeds of up to 70 m/min, and the high-speed-extruded alloys have excellent mechanical properties. This makes them ideal materials for weight reduction in transportation industries such as automotive, aerospace, and urban air mobility.​
