Room-Temperature-Processed Perovskite Solar Cells Surpass 24% Efficiency
Room-temperature perovskite solar cells (PSCs) offer a promising solution for improving solar power efficiency and scalability. A team led by Professor Hyo Jung Kim from the Department of Organic Material Science and Engineering developed a method to produce high-quality perovskite films at room temperature, achieving 24.4% efficiency. This breakthrough eliminates high-temperature processing, ensuring stable phase conversion and enhanced performance. The study also demonstrates the potential for using PSCs on unconventional substrates, opening up new opportunities for versatile, sustainable solar applications.

Novel Compact Meta-Silencer Design for Efficient and Low-Frequency Noise Reduction
Traditional silencers are often used to reduce low-frequency noise, which may affect mental health, causing stress and anxiety. However, silencers require bulky designs for effective noise reduction. Now, a team of researchers led by Professor Kyungjun Song from the Department of Mechanical Engineering developed a compact meta-silencer that uses coiled multi-slits to efficiently attenuate broadband low-frequency noise. By leveraging acoustic black hole and rainbow trapping effects, this innovative design offers a more space-efficient solution for noise mitigation. This study provides new possibilities for noise control in applications like aviation and urban environments, improving quality of life.

Exploring the Risk of Suicide Across Countries on Holidays and Weekdays
While suicide is a leading global cause of death, most of the studies report only temporal variations in suicide risk in Western countries. To bridge this gap, a research team led by Professor Whanhee Lee​ from the School of Biomedical Convergence Engineering​ investigated variations in suicide risk across different days of the week and national holidays. The study found that Mondays and New Year’s Day are associated with the highest suicide risk. These findings provide valuable insights to help develop more effective and targeted suicide prevention strategies.

Biomarkers for Peri-Implantitis Diagnosis and Treatment
Peri-implantitis, a chronic inflammatory disease affecting dental implants, is associated with a high rate of recurrence due to a lack of insights into its pathogenesis. A research team led by Professor Yun Hak Kim from the School of Medicine and Professor Hyun-Joo Kim from the School of Dentistry identified key biomarker genes overexpressed in peri-implantitis, distinguishing it from periodontitis. These biomarkers, including ACTA2, FAP, and PDGFRβ, have the potential to improve diagnosis and help develop targeted treatments. This breakthrough holds potential for better management of peri-implantitis and may reduce recurrence rates by providing more personalized and effective therapies.

Monitoring the Atomic-Scale Transformation in Infinite-Layer Transition Metal Oxides
Infinite-layer transition metal oxides exhibit intriguing magnetic properties due to strong orbital hybridization. A research team led by Professor Jaekwang Lee from the Department of Physics utilized in situ atomic-resolution electron microscopy to investigate the atomic mechanisms behind the transformation of SrFeO2.5 to SrFeO2. The study revealed anisotropic oxygen release, governed by lattice reorientation, and provided insights into the reconfiguration of oxygen coordination. These findings may enhance the understanding and development of similar materials, including cuprate and nickelate superconductors.
